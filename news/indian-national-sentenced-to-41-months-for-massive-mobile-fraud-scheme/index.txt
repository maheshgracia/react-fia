f:T8d4,<p dir="ltr"><strong>Dhananjay Singh gets 41 months in prison and is ordered to pay $10M for leading a massive six-year international cell phone scam</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">A major multi-year operation that bled millions of dollars from cellular providers and insurance firms has reached its conclusion in federal court. Dhananjay Singh, a 35-year-old Indian national, was sentenced on April 21, 2026, to 41 months in prison for his leadership role in a sprawling identity theft and mail fraud conspiracy.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The sentence, handed down by U.S. District Judge Madeline Cox Arleo, follows Singh&rsquo;s earlier guilty plea to charges of conspiracy to commit mail fraud and the interstate transfer of stolen property.</p>
<p dir="ltr">According to investigators, the scheme operated for six years, spanning from 2013 - 2019. Singh and his network utilized a sophisticated "ghost" infrastructure of stolen and fabricated identities to file thousands of fraudulent insurance claims for "lost" or "damaged" high-end mobile devices.</p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Dhananjay Singh gets 41 months in federal prison&nbsp;</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">By exploiting the replacement policies of major carriers, the group successfully triggered the shipment of thousands of new devices through the U.S. mail. To avoid detection, the conspirators maintained a vast network of mailboxes and storage facilities throughout New Jersey and across the country.&nbsp;</p>
<p dir="ltr">Once the replacement phones arrived at these dead-drops, they were consolidated and shipped overseas to be sold on the international black market. The scale of the fraud was staggering, with federal prosecutors estimating total losses of $10 million. Further, he is ordered to pay $10 million in restitution, Singh also faces three years of court-monitored supervision.</p>
<p dir="ltr">U.S. Attorney Robert Frazer lauded the inter-agency cooperation that brought Singh to justice, noting that the probe required the combined resources of the FBI, Homeland Security, and the U.S. Postal Inspection Service. The case, handled by the Bank Integrity and Money Laundering Unit, serves as a sharp warning to those leveraging international logistics for domestic fraud.</p>
