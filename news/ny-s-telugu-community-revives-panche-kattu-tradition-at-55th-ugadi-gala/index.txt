f:T712,<p dir="ltr"><strong>Over 60 participants showcase authentic handloom dhotis in New York to preserve regional Telugu identity</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">In a powerful display of cultural resilience, the New York Telugu community transformed the 55th anniversary of the Telugu Literary and Cultural Association (TLCA) into a masterclass on tradition. Amidst the high-energy Ugadi 2026 celebrations, the focus shifted from the modern skyline to the timeless elegance of "Panche Kattu," as a massive men&rsquo;s fashion walk brought the authentic Telugu dhoti back to the forefront of the diaspora's identity.&nbsp;</p>
<p dir="ltr">Over 60 participants took to the stage, not in modern suits, but in authentic handloom attire. Spearheaded by a dedicated group known as the &lsquo;Jawanies&rsquo;, featuring Nehru Kataru, Kiran Reddy Parvatala, and their team, the initiative aimed to rescue the traditional Telugu draping style from fading into obscurity.&nbsp;</p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Telugu Panche Takes Center Stage in Cultural Showcase&nbsp;</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">While the dhoti remains a staple at weddings, the specific regional nuances of the Telugu wrap are often lost to Indo-Western trends or simplified versions from other states. Organizers, including Sirisha Tunuguntla, emphasized that this wasn't just a catwalk; it was a cultural intervention.</p>
<p dir="ltr">By showcasing the elegance of the original "panche," the event sought to bridge the gap for the younger diaspora in America, many of whom are distant from these sartorial roots. The &lsquo;Jawanies&rsquo; expressed gratitude to the TLCA leadership and donors for making this preservation effort possible, hoping that the visual impact of 60 men in handlooms will spark a renewed pride in regional identity.</p>
